Go-to-Market · Data Science B2B Software Seller Productivity

Sellers spend most of the week not selling.
Here’s the intelligence layer that gives it back.

Across B2B software, reps spend under a third of their time in front of customers — the rest drains into prospecting, research, CRM, and prep. An AI-powered Sales Intelligence Framework attacks both sides at once: it strips out the non-customer-facing work and points every seller at the right account, the right person, and the right pitch — lifting win rate, not just saving time.

Wenlong Song Head of Mid-Market Data Science, Intuit June 2026 ~16 min read

There are only two ways to grow a sales number: add more selling capacity, or make the capacity you already have convert better. Most organizations reach for the first. The cheaper, faster lever is the second — and it’s hiding in plain sight, in the majority of the week your sellers spend not selling.

A seller’s job, in theory, is to talk to customers. In practice, the day is dominated by everything around the conversation: deciding who to call, stitching together an account’s story across five tabs, hunting for the right contact, writing the follow-up, logging it all in the CRM. That work is necessary, but it isn’t selling — and it’s where the hours go.

Over the last few years I’ve built the system that takes that work back. Not a chatbot bolted onto the CRM, but a layered Sales Intelligence Framework that decides what matters, explains why, and delivers it inside the tools sellers already use. This is how it’s built — and why it moves win rate, not just minutes.

01 · The problem

The most expensive thing in sales is a seller not selling

Start with the number that should bother every revenue leader: in Salesforce’s State of Sales research, reps report spending under 30% of their time actually selling. The other 70% — prospecting research, data entry, internal meetings, prep, follow-up coordination — is overhead. You are paying a fully loaded sales team to spend two days out of three on work that, done differently, a system could do for them.

And it isn’t spread evenly. The friction clusters at specific points along the seller’s workflow. Walk the journey end to end and the same pattern repeats: at each hand-off, the seller stops to find, research, assemble, or re-enter something a machine already knows.

None of these stages is broken in isolation. The problem is cumulative: a hundred small frictions, repeated across every account and every rep, every week. That is why the answer can’t be one clever tool — it has to be a layer that runs underneath the entire workflow.

02 · The diagnosis

Key frictions affecting seller productivity

Pull together the external research and what I’ve seen inside a large mid-market sales org, and the drains on productivity collapse into a short, stubborn list. These are the things that actually move the needle — or, today, block it:

  • The time tax. Under a third of the week is customer-facing; the rest is admin, prep, and prospecting. The single biggest lever in the whole function is simply giving that time back.
  • Tool sprawl & context-switching. Sellers hop across a stack of disconnected systems — CRM, engagement, dialer, marketing, data vendors. Every switch is lost context and a chance for the data to disagree with itself.
  • Blank-page prep & manual prospecting. Deciding who to work and reconstructing why is done by hand, account by account, with no prioritization underneath it.
  • Slow follow-up. Speed-to-lead is decisive — the first credible responder wins a wildly disproportionate share — yet follow-up is manual and easily dropped.
  • Generic messaging. One pitch for everyone. The best reps tailor to the account’s specific need; most don’t have the time, so resonance suffers and win rate plateaus.
  • Admin & CRM drag. Note-taking and data entry are consistently named the most time-consuming tasks in the job — and they produce no revenue.
  • Broken hand-offs. Signed contracts stall before provisioning; onboarding is misaligned; the seller never gets a clean signal back to expand the account.

The consequences show up in the numbers. Per the 2025 Ebsta × Pavilion GTM Benchmarks, win rates have slid to 19% (from 29% the prior year), and a startling 14% of sellers now drive 80% of revenue. That isn’t a talent gap — it’s a leverage gap. The top performers have figured out how to spend their hours on the right accounts with the right message. The framework’s job is to hand that leverage to everyone.

Here’s the encouraging part. The opportunity is real. The question is architecture: what do you actually build?

03 · The answer

One framework, aimed at both halves of the problem

Most “AI for sales” initiatives pick one friction and build a point solution: a better dialer, a smarter content tool, a forecasting model. Useful, but they add to the tool sprawl they were meant to cure. The framework takes the opposite stance: a single intelligence layer that runs under the whole workflow, mapping each friction to the capability that dissolves it.

The design goal is deliberately two-sided. Every capability has to do both jobs — remove non-customer-facing work and improve the odds of winning. A feature that saves time but doesn’t lift win rate is a convenience; a feature that lifts win rate but costs the seller an hour is a tax. We only ship the ones that do both.

04 · The architecture

Three layers: foundation, engine, and the seller’s surface

The framework is built in three layers, and the order matters. Each layer is useless without the one beneath it and invisible without the one above it. Data with no intelligence is a warehouse no one reads; intelligence with no surface is a dashboard no one opens.

Layer 1 — the Data Foundation

Everything starts with one well-governed source of truth, enriched at two grains. At the company/account level: firmographics; first-party product usage; third-party tool and competitor-product usage; Voice of Customer; purchase intent; and the high-value change signals that predict a buying window — a key personnel change (a new CFO), or a key business-need change (a new investment, an acquisition, a move into a new market). At the user/contact level: role and title, individual product usage, and the full history of how that person has engaged with marketing and sales. Get this layer right and the models above it get simple; get it wrong and no amount of modeling saves you.

Layer 2 — the Intelligence Engine

This is where data becomes a decision. Three models, each answering a different question:

  • Lead Scoring Framework — for inbound and marketing-sourced contacts: which leads are worth a seller’s time, now? It blends account propensity, contact-level engagement, and behavioral signals to rank and route leads instead of dumping them, undifferentiated, into a queue.
  • Account Scoring Framework — for the outbound/install-base motion: which accounts should I work, for which product, and why? This is the centerpiece, and it earns its own section below.
  • Contact Intelligence — once you’ve picked the account: who inside it do I actually call? It predicts each contact’s persona (C-suite, finance, accountant…) and the likelihood they’ll engage, so reps reach the decision-maker instead of the first email they find. Multi-threading to the right people is one of the strongest known win-rate levers — Ebsta finds engaging six or more stakeholders early can lift win rate from ~12% to 40%+.

Layer 3 — Seller-Facing Tools

Intelligence only counts if it reaches the seller without friction — so it ships inside the tools they already live in (Salesforce, Marketo, Amazon Connect, Sell), never as a separate dashboard to remember to check:

  • Prospecting App — the ranked list of leads and accounts to work, with filters and one-click actions.
  • Intelligence Card — the account’s story on one card: key attributes, the product recommendation and its reasoning, a talk track, and contact intelligence — surfaced on the account, lead, and opportunity pages.
  • Automated Work Queue — the sequenced next-best-action list (its own section below).
  • Email Drafting — personalized outreach drafted from the account’s signals, ready to review and send.
  • Demo Content Creation — tailored demo material assembled from the recommendation, so the demo speaks to this account’s situation.

05 · Deep dive

From a score to a framework

Most organizations already have an account-propensity model. It outputs a number: this account is an 0.84. Useful — a ranked list beats a random one — but a number alone leaves the seller to answer every question that actually matters. A propensity score tells you who is hot. A scoring framework tells you what to do about it.

That distinction is the entire point of the Account Scoring Framework. For every account it produces not one output but five: a calibrated score, the best product to sell next, the potential value of that sale, the reason why (the signals behind the call), and the talk track for how to open the conversation. And because it’s a framework rather than a single model, it can bend to business priorities — if the company strategically wants to push one product over another this quarter, the framework weighs that in when it picks the recommendation.

How it’s built

The architecture is what makes those five outputs trustworthy and consistent. Rather than one monolithic model, the framework is a pipeline that ends in a single standardized output every seller surface can consume:

  • Unified feature mart. One governed source of truth — firmographics, product-feature usage, competitor and third-party signals, in-product behavior — so every model downstream reads from the same place.
  • Per-product propensity models. One model per product, each built by the team closest to that product’s motion (a mix of gradient-boosted ML and transparent rule-based models). Modularity means a model can be improved or swapped without breaking anything downstream.
  • The framework layer. The orchestration that turns many models into one answer: a gatekeeper that holds every model to standards on accuracy and explainability; cross-product ranking that picks the best next product by expected incremental value when several compete; a business-priority override for strategic weighting; and an LLM that writes the talk track from the winning signals.
  • Standardized output. Every account resolves to the same compact schema — score · key reason · next-best product · talk track — refreshed daily and published once.
  • Seller surfaces & feedback loop. That output flows into the Intelligence Card, the Prospecting App, and campaign routing — and outcomes flow back to measure lift and retrain.

The “so what” is simple. A propensity score makes a seller faster at guessing. The framework removes the guess: it hands over the account, the product, the value, the reason, and the opening line — which is exactly the prep work that used to eat the morning.

06 · Deep dive

The Automated Work Queue — and the last mile

Stack the layers together and you get the most valuable seller-facing surface of all: a queue that assembles the seller’s day for them. The rep opens their laptop in the morning and the intelligence has already decided what to do, in what order, and why — each item a single card with a propensity score, the potential value, the context, the plan, and one button to act. This is the ultimate form of the framework: not a report to interpret, but a sequence of actions to execute.

The honest part: the last mile is integration

I want to be precise about what’s real and what’s still ahead, because the gap is instructive. The intelligence layer is ready — the scoring, the contact ranking, the talk tracks, the sequencing logic all exist and run. Today we’ve already automated the flow end-to-end for specific actions, such as calling a high-propensity account: the system picks the account, surfaces the plan, and the seller acts.

What blocks the fully autonomous queue — every action type, one click, start to finish — isn’t the intelligence. It’s tool integration. Sales teams run different tools for lead engagement versus account engagement, and different tools again for calling versus email. The queue can recommend the next action perfectly; handing that action off to the right execution tool, with state synced back, is the unsolved part. We have the brain; the last mile is wiring it to the hands.

The intelligence layer is the hard part, and it’s done. The last mile to autonomous selling is unglamorous tool integration — and that’s exactly why it’s the next thing to build.

07 · Recommendation

Where to start

You don’t build this all at once, and you don’t start with the flashy part. The sequence that compounds:

1

Unify the data first. One profile per account and contact. A modest model on clean, unified data beats a brilliant one on fragmented data — every time.

2

Turn the score into a framework. Ship the reason, the product, the value, and the talk track alongside the number. Guidance, not just a ranking.

3

Put it where sellers live. Surface everything inside the CRM and engagement tools. If it’s a separate dashboard, adoption dies.

4

Sequence the work. Stand up the work queue for the action types you can wire end-to-end today — prove the motion before you scale it.

5

Close the last mile. Prioritize the tool integrations that let the queue execute, not just recommend. That’s the road to autonomous selling.

The prize is two-sided, and that’s what makes it worth doing. Take the non-customer-facing work off the seller’s plate and you give back the scarcest resource in the function — selling time. Aim that reclaimed time at the right account, the right person, and the right pitch, and you don’t just save hours — you raise the odds of winning. Most “sales productivity” tools pick one of those. The intelligence framework is built to deliver both, from the same foundation — and that combination is how a whole sales force starts to sell like its best rep.
