ARTICLE 03 · DATA & AI PLATFORM Platform Architecture Agentic AI

The AI-Ready Analytics Platform
Architecture Framework

For years, analytics meant a Data Scientist at the end of a pipeline. Agentic AI rewrites the architecture — and who it serves.

Wenlong Song Head of Mid-Market Data Science, Intuit June 2026

01 · The Problem

Analytics Built for One End User

For years, the analytics platform served exactly one persona: the Data Scientist.

The architecture was a linear pipeline, and it barely changed for a decade. Raw data moved from operational systems into a database, and from there Data Scientists produced the outputs everyone else consumed — dashboards, reports, slide decks, spreadsheets, and ML models. Every insight required a human expert in the loop, scoping the question, writing the query, interpreting the result, and packaging it for the next audience.

This architecture worked — so long as you accepted its two hard constraints:

  • Ingestion was limited to structured data. Relational tables, event logs, CRM exports. Unstructured data — customer emails, support tickets, Slack threads, call transcripts, sales notes — couldn’t be processed at scale, so it simply wasn’t. The richest signal in the enterprise stayed on the floor.
  • The Data Scientist was the platform’s single end user. Product Managers, Marketers, Sellers, and Customer Success Managers consumed outputs; they couldn’t interact with the platform directly. Every question traveled through a DS queue.

The business implication is blunt: analytics throughput was capped at headcount. A team of ten Data Scientists could only answer ten queues’ worth of questions per sprint. And the majority of enterprise signal — Voice of Customer, unstructured documents, call transcripts — was systematically ignored because the pipeline had no way to ingest it.

Agentic AI breaks both constraints. But seizing that opportunity requires a fundamentally different architecture — one designed from the start to serve agents and humans, structured and unstructured data.

02 · The Framework

The AI-Ready Analytics Platform Architecture Framework

AAPAF is a four-layer architecture that describes how a modern analytics platform must be built to serve both human analysts and autonomous AI agents.

Each layer has a distinct role, and they compose in sequence:

  • Foundation — the raw material: all data sources (structured and unstructured) plus the context sources (metric definitions, analytics codebase, business wiki) that give data meaning.
  • Analytics Assets — the intermediate layer that transforms raw foundation data into trusted, reusable assets: an Analytics-Ready Datamart (owned by Data Engineering) and a Semantic Layer (owned by Data Science), plus activation assets — Agentic Analytics Skills, a Knowledge Graph, and Propensity Models — that package intelligence for agents and products.
  • Analytics Products — the five consumer-facing products built on the assets: KPI Dashboards, Agentic Data Analytics, Product Growth Intelligence, Recommendation Systems, and the Customer 360 Hub.
  • End Users — the seven personas the platform now serves directly: Data Scientists, Product Managers, Marketers, Executives, Customers, Sellers, and Customer Success Managers.

03 · Implementation Challenges

Seven Hard Problems in Practice

The framework is conceptually clean. Implementation is not. Here are the challenges every team hits, and what it takes to get past them.

1. Processing Unstructured Data at Scale

Emails, call transcripts, JIRA tickets, and Slack threads are the richest source of customer truth — and historically the most ignored. Ingesting, chunking, embedding, and keeping unstructured content fresh at enterprise volume requires dedicated pipeline infrastructure, not a one-time ETL job. What it takes: a purpose-built unstructured data pipeline with incremental refresh, deduplication, and quality gates before any downstream asset consumes it.

2. Building and Governing the Semantic Layer

A Semantic Layer is only as valuable as the business agrees it is. If Marketing’s definition of “active customer” diverges from Sales’, the layer becomes a source of conflict rather than alignment. What it takes: a cross-functional metric council, version-controlled definitions, and a clear ownership model (Data Science authors; business teams ratify).

3. Constructing and Maintaining the Knowledge Graph

A Knowledge Graph links entities — accounts, contacts, products, needs, interactions — so agents can reason about relationships, not just retrieve facts. Building the initial graph is hard; keeping it current as the business evolves is harder. What it takes: automated entity resolution, scheduled graph refresh tied to the Datamart update cycle, and explicit ownership of graph schema changes.

4. Agent Reliability, Evaluation, and Guardrails

An agentic analytics product that occasionally hallucinates metrics destroys trust faster than no product at all. Evaluation is non-trivial: agents must be tested on ambiguous questions, edge cases, and adversarial inputs. What it takes: an eval harness with ground-truth query pairs, confidence thresholds that route uncertain answers to human review, and a continuous red-teaming loop before any agent sees production traffic.

5. DE / DS Ownership Boundaries

The Datamart is Data Engineering’s domain; the Semantic Layer and Activation Assets are Data Science’s. Without explicit handoff contracts, teams step on each other: DS queries raw tables, DE hard-codes business logic. What it takes: a written interface contract (what DE delivers, at what SLA, in what schema) and a shared data-quality SLO that both teams are accountable to.

6. Adoption Beyond Data Scientists

The platform’s value proposition is that it serves PMs, Marketers, Sellers, and CSMs directly — but these personas won’t adopt a tool they don’t trust or understand. What it takes: embedded onboarding (tooltips, example questions, guardrailed query templates), a feedback loop back to the DS team, and executive sponsorship that signals the platform is the expected way to get answers — not a nice-to-have.

7. Trust and Data-Access Control

A Customer 360 Hub that surfaces sensitive account data to the wrong Seller, or an Agentic Analytics product that exposes HR metrics to a junior analyst, is a compliance failure waiting to happen. Agents inherit the access-control problem and make it harder to audit. What it takes: row- and column-level security enforced at the Datamart layer (not the product layer), clear data-classification policies, and agent session logging with full query provenance.

04 · Where to Start

Build in Sequence, Not in Parallel

The biggest mistake teams make is trying to build the Knowledge Graph before the Datamart is clean, or launching an Agentic Analytics product before the Semantic Layer is trusted. The architecture has a natural build sequence — respect it.

1

Foundation first. Audit your data sources. Prioritize structured data pipelines (Product, CRM, 3rd Party) and establish a clear home for unstructured data (VoC, documents). Codify your Context Sources: start with a metric definitions document, even if it’s just a shared spreadsheet. Without a trusted foundation, every layer above it is built on sand.

2

Datamart + Semantic Layer. Data Engineering builds an Analytics-Ready Datamart with agreed-upon SLAs. Data Science layers a Semantic Layer on top — the first version only needs the 10–20 metrics that answer 80% of recurring questions. Get both teams to sign the interface contract before activation work begins.

3

Activation Assets. Build Agentic Analytics Skills (callable tools for LLM agents) from the Semantic Layer. Then construct the Knowledge Graph from the Datamart and Semantic Layer combined. Propensity Models follow once you have clean, conformed data to train on. Each asset is independently deployable; ship them incrementally.

4

Analytics Products. KPI Dashboards can launch directly off the Datamart — ship these first to demonstrate value while the AI assets mature. Agentic Data Analytics requires the Skills and Knowledge Graph. Recommendation Systems require the Knowledge Graph and Propensity Models. Customer 360 Hub and Product Growth Intelligence compose from the full stack.

5

Drive adoption. Identify one champion in each persona group (a PM who asks data questions daily, a Seller who would use a smart lead queue). Build with them, not for them. Measure platform engagement — questions asked, products opened, insights acted on — not just data pipeline SLAs. Platform value is realized at the End User layer, not the Foundation layer.

“The platform's job is no longer to produce answers for analysts. It's to make every person in the business — and every agent acting on their behalf — analytically capable.”

Recommendation

Start a Foundation audit this quarter. Identify the three most-requested unanswered questions in your business and work backwards: which data sources, which assets, which products would answer them. That gap analysis is your roadmap. The teams that build this architecture now will have a durable competitive advantage as agentic AI moves from experiment to operating system.
