Go-to-Market · Data Science B2B Software Upsell & Cross-sell

Stop targeting who looks likely.
Start targeting what customers need.

Most B2B targeting rests on firmographics, intent, and engagement — signals that correlate with buying but don’t cause it. Here’s a Need-Centric Targeting Framework that reverse-engineers a product’s value into causal buying signals — and the expansion it unlocks across an existing install base.

Wenlong Song Head of Mid-Market Data Science, Intuit June 2026 ~12 min read

Every dollar of net-new logo growth is hard-won. But for most B2B software companies, the fastest, highest-margin growth is already sitting inside the house — in the customers you already won. The question is no longer “who should we acquire?” It’s “which of our existing customers is ready for more, and why?”

The honest answer is that the industry’s standard targeting playbook is surprisingly bad at the second question. It is built to find accounts that resemble buyers, not accounts that need what we sell next. That gap is exactly where I’ve spent the last few years — and the framework below is what came out of it.

Here’s what you’ll learn

  • Why the standard targeting stack — firmographics + intent + engagement — has a built-in win-rate ceiling, and why it’s still worth running.
  • How a Need-Centric Targeting Framework (NTF) reverse-engineers a product’s differentiated value into signals with a real causal link to the buying decision.
  • The method I used to discover needs at scale — mining tens of thousands of sales conversations with GenAI.
  • How to prove a need actually drives purchase, using experiments and propensity-score matching — not correlations.
  • How one need data asset powers three GTM plays: high-propensity target lists, personalized talk tracks, and need-based ICPs.

01 · Where every GTM strategy begins

Targeting is the first move — and the ICP is the opening

Ask any go-to-market team where strategy starts and you’ll hear the same answer: define the target. Before a dollar of media or a single seller’s hour is spent, you decide who you are for. That definition usually takes the form of an Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) — a description of the accounts most likely to buy, stay, and grow.

Take a category leader like HubSpot. A simplified version of its ICP might read: B2B companies with roughly 25–200 employees, an active marketing function, a steady flow of inbound web traffic, and a patchwork of disconnected point tools they’ve outgrown. That profile is useful. It tells media where to spend and sellers whom to call first. It is also, almost entirely, a description of fit — of who an account is, not of what it is trying to do that it currently can’t.

That distinction is the whole story. Fit is necessary, but it is rarely the reason a deal closes.

02 · The problem

The standard targeting stack — and why it underperforms

Walk into almost any B2B software company and the targeting stack — whether it’s a manual ICP or a sophisticated account-propensity model — is built on three families of signal:

  • Firmographics (mostly third-party): industry, employee count, revenue, tech stack, location. The “who.”
  • Intent signals (first- and third-party): clicks on an in-product or web ad, a demo request, or third-party data showing the account is searching certain keywords across the web. The “maybe-now.”
  • Product-engagement signals (first-party): login frequency, seats active, number of features touched. The “how busy.”

The best teams layer these into a single score — fit × intent × win-probability — and the highest-fidelity programs blend all three. This works. It is cheap to build, it scales, and it beats spraying the whole base. I want to be clear about that: a correlational model is far better than no model at all.

But it has a ceiling, and the ceiling is structural. None of these signals is a direct causal driver of the purchase decision. Firmographics answer who but miss why and when. Intent proxies — ad clicks, keyword surges — are noisy, late, and easily confused with idle curiosity. Engagement measures activity, not unmet need: a power user who is perfectly happy looks identical to one who is about to hit a wall.

Every one of these signals correlates with buying. None of them is the reason a customer buys.

Because the inputs only correlate with the decision, the model’s precision is capped no matter how much data you pour in — and you feel it as a win rate that plateaus, message after message that doesn’t resonate, and sellers who quietly fall back on their own discovery.

03 · A different question

Enter the Need-Centric Targeting Framework

So I flipped the question. Instead of “which accounts look like buyers?” the framework asks “which customers have a need our product uniquely solves — right now?”

Segmentation and firmographics define the WHO. A need-centric lens adds the WHY and the WHEN — and those two are what actually move a deal. The shift sounds almost obvious once you say it out loud. The hard part is operational: how do you identify a customer’s need without talking to them?

04 · How I got here

The idea came from listening to what closers actually do

For years, analyzing sales conversations at scale was impossible — the data was unstructured and the volume was crushing. That changed. As GenAI matured, my team could read tens of thousands of sales-call transcripts with high accuracy and pull structure out of them.

When I dug into how our best reps were winning upgrades and attach products, the pattern was almost monotonous. They didn’t open with the product. They opened with the need:

“Tell me about something you’re trying to do today that your current setup won’t let you.”

Then — and only then — they pitched, framing the new capability entirely around the gap the customer had just described. It’s completely logical. People don’t buy features; they buy a way out of a problem. The winning motion was always explore the need → map it to a capability → pitch to the need.

That gave me the thread to pull. If a great rep can uncover a need in conversation, then the need leaves traces — in how the customer actually uses the product. The job was to find those traces in data and read them the way a great rep reads a room.

05 · The mechanism

Reverse-engineering value into causal signals

Here is the core method. Rather than start from data we happen to have, NTF starts from the differentiated value a product delivers and works backward to the data:

  • Begin with a differentiated value the product offers (something competitors or the lower tier can’t match).
  • Identify the feature that delivers that value.
  • Find the data signal directly linked to needing that feature — a behavior a customer only exhibits when they have the underlying problem.
  • Treat that signal as evidence of an inferred need, which in turn drives purchase propensity.

The governing principle: the more direct the link between the signal and the feature, the stronger the causal effect. A generic “high engagement” flag is a weak, distant proxy. “This customer repeatedly does by hand the exact thing our feature automates” is a near-causal one.

A worked example

QuickBooks Advanced offers a backup & restore capability — differentiated value for a business that can’t afford to lose its books. What data signal directly implies that need? A high volume of manual adjustments to the books, made by multiple users. A team editing the ledger by hand, in parallel, is one bad entry away from a costly mistake — precisely the pain backup & restore removes. That behavior isn’t a vague proxy; it is the need, expressed in product telemetry.

The signal is the behavior a customer only exhibits when they actually have the underlying problem — so it is near-causal, not coincidental.

The same recipe generalizes. Every differentiated capability maps to a behavior a customer performs only when they have the matching problem — and a behavioral threshold turns that raw signal into a need:

06 · The proof

Proving it: causal inference, not correlation

A signal that looks like a need still has to earn its place. This is the step that separates NTF from a dressed-up propensity model: before a need enters the framework, we test whether it causally moves the outcome — with experiments where we can run them (holdouts, A/B tests, uplift modeling) and quasi-experimental methods where we can’t (propensity-score matching, difference-in-differences, controlling for firmographics). Needs that wash out under controls get pruned; needs that survive get weighted by how much they actually move the number.

Example 1 — Need → upgrade

For one flagship product we isolated a set of customer needs and tested each against the decision to upgrade. Critically, the causal cut controlled for firmographics, industry, and revenue; a plain correlation does not. Both views pointed the same way, which gave us confidence — but the causal view is what we trusted for weighting. Three findings shaped everything downstream:

  • Needs are not equal. The strongest needs (e.g., hitting user limits, manual workflow toil) drove on the order of a ~5× higher upgrade rate (illustrative); the weakest barely moved it — so we stopped treating them the same.
  • More needs, more conversion. Upgrade likelihood roughly doubled the moment a customer had even one verified need, then climbed almost linearly with each additional need (illustrative).
  • Needs compound. Certain pairs of needs converted far above either need alone — the combination, not just the count, carries signal.

Example 2 — Feature adoption → retention

Expansion isn’t worth much if it doesn’t stick, so we ran the same discipline on retention, using propensity-score matching to build an apples-to-apples comparison — matching adopters to non-adopters on tenure, firmographics, and early engagement to strip out selection bias and isolate the causal value of adoption. The pattern was sharp: crossing a threshold of adopted capabilities produced a double-digit-percentage-point lift in one-year retention (illustrative), and for brand-new customers, getting to the first meaningful capability in the first 30 days sharply cut early churn.

The takeaway compounds the whole thesis: the same need signals that predict who will buy more also predict who will stay — so one asset serves acquisition, expansion, and retention.

07 · The payoff

One data asset, three GTM plays

The output of all this isn’t a one-off model — it’s a durable need data asset: a single source-of-truth table that, for every customer, records which needs they have, how intense each is, and how much each causally matters. That asset powers three plays at once.

Play 1 — A need score for high-propensity target lists

Roll a customer’s needs into a single need score and you get a ranked, high-propensity target list that Marketing and Sales can act on tomorrow. The score isn’t a black box. It’s built from the causal weights and breadth effect we measured: each need is weighted by its causal importance (the lift it actually drives, not a hand-set weight), behavioral thresholds turn raw signals into needs (e.g., 40+ manual transactions a month), and a breadth multiplier rewards customers who have several active needs — who convert well above those with a single, even strong, one.

Play 2 — Personalized content and talk tracks

Because every score is explainable down to the specific need, the messaging writes itself. Marketers build benefit-specific content and offers for each need cohort — not a generic “efficiency” campaign, but “you create 40+ invoices by hand every month; here’s the hour you get back.” Sellers get the customer’s needs before the call, plus a talk track tuned to them — the same explore-then-pitch motion our best reps already run, now handed to everyone.

Play 3 — Need-based ICPs and a play per ICP

Finally, the asset lets us redraw the ICP itself — not by firmographics, but by need archetype. “Multi-user teams editing the books by hand” is a sharper, more actionable ICP than “50–200 employees in professional services,” because it names the why. Each need-based ICP gets its own GTM play: the right channel, offer, and motion for that specific problem.

Step back and you can see this as a maturity ladder. Most teams start with generic benefit campaigns, graduate to propensity-based targeting, and — if they get there — reach need-centric targeting, which is the only rung that targets the reason a customer buys:

08 · Recommendation

Where to start

You don’t need to boil the ocean. The framework compounds, so the right move is to prove it on one product’s most differentiated value and let the win pull the next investment:

1

Pick one differentiated value. Choose a capability your top tier has and the rest of the market doesn’t.

2

Build the value → signal map. Name the behavior a customer only shows when they have the matching problem, and set its threshold.

3

Validate one need causally. Run a holdout or matched comparison. If the need doesn’t move the outcome under controls, cut it.

4

Ship a need-scored list. Hand Sales and Marketing a ranked cohort with the reason attached — and a talk track to match.

5

Measure lift vs. the propensity baseline. Hold out a slice scored the old way. Let the win-rate delta fund the next need.

For most of the last decade, inferring need at scale was a fantasy — the evidence was locked inside unstructured conversations no one could read in bulk. GenAI changed the economics, and that’s why need-centric targeting is newly practical rather than merely appealing. The companies that win the next cycle of expansion won’t be the ones with the biggest list of look-alike accounts. They’ll be the ones who can answer, for every customer they already have, a deceptively simple question: what do they need next — and how do we know?
